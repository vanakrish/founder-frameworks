This skill helps with positioning against competitors, competitive moats, and market warfare.

**Bill Gates — Relentless, wants it all:**
- "He is utterly relentless. He is indefatigable. He is absolutely focused and he wants it all." (Ellison on Gates)
- Studied every competitor obsessively — knew their CEOs, revenues, current projects
- Kept photo of competitor on desk: "destroy them"
- "Bill wants people to think he is Thomas Edison when he is really Rockefeller" — not the inventor, the business strategist
- Preferred to win the war over winning the argument
- "I'd rather win the war than win the argument. He doesn't care where the idea comes from as long as he's the one who gets paid for it."

**Larry Ellison — Pick your enemies carefully:**
- "We pick our enemies very carefully. It helps us focus."
- Positioned Oracle against Microsoft AND IBM simultaneously — chose the biggest enemies to establish legitimacy
- Narrative creates reality: "Network computing architecture got no traction. Internet computing architecture got traction. Same product, different name."
- "You can't get rich doing what everyone else is doing"
- Contrarian positioning: when everyone said databases were dead, he bet the company on them
- Simplify what competitors make complex: "If Detroit ran like Silicon Valley, nobody would sell cars. They'd sell parts."

**Jensen Huang — Create markets, don't compete:**
- "Where there are no customers, there are also no competitors. That is a 0 billion dollar market."
- "We build Ferraris. I don't want to let someone come in and be the price leader."
- Ship the Whole Cow: repackage waste products as cheaper lines to block competitors from climbing the value ladder
- "Complacency kills" — your biggest competitor is your own success
- "Make no mistake, we need to kill Intel before they kill us" — direct, named competition
- Create the market (GPU compute for AI) so you own the category definition

**Dietrich Mateschitz — Become the category:**
- Red Bull didn't enter the energy drink market — it CREATED the category
- "The most dangerous thing for a branded product is low interest" — obscurity is worse than competition
- Don't buy ads, create content — own the media that your audience consumes
- Sports teams as competitive moats: "In value terms, the total editorial media value plus the media assets are superior to pure advertising"
- Self-ironic, non-conformist brand positioning attracts the demographic you want through culture, not targeting

**Process:**
1. Ask: Who is your competitor? What's the competitive dynamic? (Head-to-head? Larger player? New entrant? Category definition?)
2. Ask: What's your current position? (Underdog? Market leader? Niche player?)
3. Apply founder lenses:
   - **Gates approach**: Study them obsessively. Know everything. Be more relentless.
   - **Ellison approach**: Pick the fight deliberately. Use narrative to reframe the market.
   - **Jensen approach**: Can you create a new market where they can't follow?
   - **Mateschitz approach**: Can you become the category itself through brand/culture?
4. Key question: Are you fighting for existing market share (Gates/Ellison) or creating a new market (Jensen/Mateschitz)?
5. Output: Competitive strategy recommendation with specific tactics + founder quotes + honest assessment of risks
